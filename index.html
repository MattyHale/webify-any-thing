Earth Observation Data Infrastructure (EODI) Standard

This document establishes a rigorous standard for Earth Observation Data Infrastructure (EODI), defining the essential capabilities that any credible EO data platform must provide. It emphasizes operational integrity over product-specific features, making clear what a high-functioning EODI shall do to support mission-critical applications.

Introduction

The standard is organized into eight core domains – Infrastructure, Security, Automation, Consistency, Governance & Compliance, Reliability & Resilience, Scalability & Performance, and Notifications & Event Triggers – outlining for each what is expected, why it matters, and the key principles of an effective system.

Eight Core Domains

1. Infrastructure

Core technical architecture underpinning the ingestion, management, and dissemination of EO data. The EODI's infrastructure must seamlessly integrate many distributed components, data sources, and partner inputs into a unified whole that behaves as a single high-functioning platform.

Key Principles:

  • Unified Data Integration: Bring together all relevant EO data sources under a common framework
  • API-First Design: Programmatic access for automation and integration
  • Multi-Constellation Support: Orchestrate data from numerous satellite missions

2. Security

Protection of sensitive data and systems through comprehensive security measures. Security is foundational – an EODI must safeguard data and operations through robust measures at every layer, including access control, encryption, and monitoring.

Key Principles:

  • Strong Authentication & Access Control: Multi-factor authentication and role-based access
  • Encryption & Data Protection: End-to-end encryption for data at rest and in transit
  • Continuous Monitoring: Audit logging and intrusion detection

3. Automation

Streamlined processes that reduce manual intervention and increase efficiency. Automation eliminates repetitive tasks, reduces human error, and enables operations at scale that would be impossible manually.

Key Principles:

  • Event-Driven Workflows: Automatic triggering of processing pipelines
  • Self-Service Capabilities: Users can discover, order, and retrieve data programmatically
  • Continuous Processing: Automated quality control and validation

4. Consistency

Standardized approaches ensuring reliable and predictable operations. Data normalization using STAC and OGC standards ensures interoperability across diverse satellite sources.

5. Governance & Compliance

Framework for managing operations within regulatory and policy requirements. Clear policies, documentation, and audit trails ensure accountability and regulatory compliance.

6. Reliability & Resilience

Systems designed to maintain operations under various conditions and recover from failures. Redundancy, failover mechanisms, and disaster recovery ensure continuous availability.

7. Scalability & Performance

Ability to handle growing data volumes and user demands efficiently. Cloud-native architecture and distributed processing enable petabyte-scale operations.

8. Notifications & Event Triggers

Real-time communication and automated responses to system events. Webhooks, alerts, and event-driven architecture keep users informed of data availability and processing status.
