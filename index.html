Introduction

Definition

Earth Observation Data Infrastructure is the layer between collection and use that standardises discovery, access, orchestration, and delivery across sensors and vendors.

This standard defines baseline requirements across eight domains:

  • Infrastructure — architecture for ingestion, storage, and delivery.
  • Security — access control, isolation, auditability.
  • Automation — orchestration from tasking to delivery.
  • Consistency — standardised metadata and outputs (e.g., STAC).
  • Governance — policy, compliance, operational guardrails.
  • Reliability — monitoring, resilience, failure handling.
  • Scalability — performance under growth and variable demand.
  • Notifications — events, triggers, downstream integration.

The standard focuses on repeatable, provider-agnostic practices so EO teams can scale operations without lock-in, manual bottlenecks, or inconsistent delivery behavior.

1. Infrastructure

The foundational layer that determines whether everything else is possible or painful.

What is Expected

The EODI's infrastructure is the core technical architecture underpinning ingestion, management, and dissemination of EO data. It should integrate distributed components, partner inputs, and multi-constellation data sources into one cohesive platform so users experience a single operating environment rather than disconnected systems.

A unified platform should remove silos and queue friction by triggering automated fulfilment end-to-end: scheduling collections, ingesting results, standardizing outputs, and delivering data reliably with low latency.

Key Infrastructure Principles

  • Unified data integration through a common catalog and metadata layer that hides source-specific quirks.
  • Self-service UI and stable APIs for ordering, tracking, and retrieval without tickets or email loops.
  • Automated, auditable fulfilment across tasking, acquisition monitoring, ingestion, preprocessing, and delivery.
  • Modular cloud-native services for catalog, ordering, processing, delivery, and identity, with independent scaling and no-downtime updates.

2. Security

Security defines whether the platform can be trusted to operate in real missions.

What is Expected

Security must be designed into every layer of the platform: strong identity controls, fine-grained authorization, encryption in transit and at rest, tenant isolation, continuous monitoring, and incident response readiness. One customer must never be able to view or affect another customer's assets.

Why It's Important

EO workloads are often sensitive or regulated. Breaches, data leaks, or silent tampering can cause operational, legal, and strategic damage. Security maturity is therefore a prerequisite for trust and deep mission integration.

Key Security Principles

  • Strong authentication and least-privilege access control with MFA, SSO, scoped tokens, and centralized identity management.
  • Encryption and data protection everywhere, backed by managed keys, vault/HSM controls, and key rotation.
  • Continuous monitoring and tested incident response with anomaly alerting, containment workflows, and audit logs.
  • Compliance and data governance controls for residency, licensing, and regional requirements enforced in code.

3. Automation

Automation is what turns a catalog into an operating system for EO programs.

What is Expected

Automation should run routine EO workflows from acquisition through delivery. Once triggered, ingestion, indexing, product generation, and fulfilment should execute through deterministic pipelines with minimal manual touchpoints.

Why It's Important

Automation increases speed, consistency, and scalability while reducing human error. It enables users to integrate the platform into their own systems without brittle manual processes.

Key Automation Principles

  • API-first design so everything possible in the UI is also scriptable with versioned, documented interfaces.
  • Workflow orchestration for retries, branching, state persistence, and observable, re-runnable execution.
  • Event-driven operations that react to data arrival, job updates, and delivery completion without polling.
  • Progressive removal of manual touchpoints so operators focus on exceptions and policy decisions.
  • User automation support via examples, SDKs, and CLIs for downstream ETL and notification integration.

4. Consistency

Consistency makes data reusable across teams, tools, and missions.

What is Expected

Consistency means data and platform behavior remain predictable regardless of source. Metadata, packaging, coordinate handling, and API response shapes should be normalized so users do not have to implement source-specific logic.

Why It's Important

Consistency reduces integration overhead and operational errors, enabling teams to build pipelines once and reuse them across providers, missions, and time windows.

Key Consistency Principles

  • Standardized formats and metadata using established standards such as STAC and cloud-optimized file formats.
  • Normalization pipelines for reprojection, format conversion, metadata translation, and quality preprocessing.
  • Uniform access behavior across collections, with stable search patterns, item shapes, links, and auth flows.
  • Quality and versioning controls with explicit lineage and transparent reprocessing updates.
  • Consistent documentation for fields, formats, errors, and examples aligned to live interfaces.

5. Governance & Compliance

Governance keeps operational speed aligned with policy, legal, and mission boundaries.

What is Expected

Governance and compliance should define and enforce who can access what, under which conditions, and for how long. These controls must be implemented directly in workflows, not left to informal policy documents.

Why It's Important

Agencies and enterprises often require demonstrable control and auditability before adoption. Strong governance reduces legal risk and ensures accountability during incidents or disputes.

Key Governance & Compliance Principles

  • Access governance with roles, groups, project scoping, approval flows, and revocable sharing.
  • License management and usage enforcement for geographic, temporal, redistribution, and expiration constraints.
  • Auditing and accountability through immutable logs, alerting, and compliance reporting views.
  • Automated lifecycle and retention controls for archival, deletion, backup validation, and integrity checks.
  • Legal and regulatory alignment for residency, export controls, and sector-specific policy enforcement.

6. Reliability & Resilience

Reliability determines whether the platform remains usable when conditions are least forgiving.

What is Expected

Reliability and resilience require high uptime, graceful degradation, and rapid recovery from failures. The platform should avoid single points of failure, replicate critical data and services, and continuously monitor health to detect issues early.

Why It's Important

EO users often depend on time-sensitive outputs. Outages or data loss during critical windows can disrupt missions and erode confidence, while resilient operations sustain trust.

Key Reliability & Resilience Principles

  • High-availability architecture with redundancy across critical layers and zero-downtime deployment practices.
  • Data redundancy and backup verification tied to documented RPO and RTO objectives.
  • Resilient processing and delivery with retries, checkpoints, alternate routes, and elastic surge handling.
  • Comprehensive monitoring and alerting for latency, errors, saturation, and user-facing SLOs.
  • Regular resilience testing through failover drills and load exercises that close identified gaps.

7. Scalability & Performance

Scalability ensures growth in users, sensors, and volume does not degrade mission outcomes.

What is Expected

Scalability and performance should hold steady as data volumes, user concurrency, and compute-heavy analytics grow. Architectures must use elastic infrastructure, geospatial-temporal indexing, caching, and distributed execution to keep queries and delivery fast at scale.

Why It's Important

Programs expand over time, and if performance collapses under load, adoption stalls. Predictable throughput and latency keep analysis timely and operational costs manageable.

Key Scalability & Performance Principles

  • Elastic resource scaling for services, workers, queues, storage, and network paths.
  • Optimized indexing and retrieval using spatial-temporal indexes, tiling, caching, and cloud-optimized formats.
  • Parallel and distributed processing that partitions workloads across clusters for faster large-job completion.
  • Continuous performance tuning through benchmarking, profiling, and release-cycle optimization.
  • Geographic distribution of services and caches to lower latency and balance regional demand.

8. Notifications & Event Triggers

Notifications connect infrastructure state changes to timely human and system action.

What is Expected

Notifications and event triggers should proactively inform users and systems when meaningful state changes occur. The platform should support channels such as email, in-app, SMS, webhooks, and queue-based delivery with user-defined filters and cadence controls.

Why It's Important

Timely alerts connect data availability to immediate action, enabling both humans and downstream systems to respond without polling delays.

Key Notifications & Event Triggers Principles

  • Real-time data alerts for ingestion, job completion, and order delivery with actionable context.
  • User-defined subscriptions and filters by saved search, geography, time, threshold, and job state.
  • Integration hooks through reliable webhooks, queues, and messaging protocols for downstream automation.
  • Reliable delivery via retries, dead-letter queues, delivery logs, and anti-flood controls.
  • Operational communications through a clear status channel for incidents and maintenance notices.

Using this reference

It is designed to support architecture decisions, operational reviews, and infrastructure planning rather than prescribe a single implementation.
